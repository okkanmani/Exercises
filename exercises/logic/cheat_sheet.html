Logical Fallacies Cheat Sheet

Back To Main Page

1. Appeal to Popularity

Definition: Saying something is true just because many people believe it.

Example: “We should go to the park because everyone in our class is going.”

2. Cause and Effect (Post Hoc)

Definition: Assuming one thing caused another just because it happened before it.

Example: “I wore my lucky socks and we won the game, so the socks must have caused the win.”

3. Strawman

Definition: Changing or oversimplifying someone’s argument to make it easier to attack.

Example: “You don’t like soccer? So you must hate all exercise!”

4. Circular Reasoning

Definition: Using the conclusion as the reason for itself.

Example: “Math is easy because it’s simple.”

5. Slippery Slope

Definition: Claiming that one small action will lead to a chain of extreme, unlikely consequences.

Example: “If we skip cleaning today, soon nothing in our house will ever be clean again!”

6. False Dilemma

Definition: Saying there are only two choices when there are actually more.

Example: “You either help me with homework or you don’t care about me.”

7. Appeal to Authority

Definition: Believing something is true just because someone important or famous said it.

Example: “We should buy this cereal because a famous singer eats it.”

8. Hasty Generalization

Definition: Making a broad claim based on too little evidence.

Example: “All tall kids are good at basketball because the tall kids in my class are good.”

9. Appeal to Tradition

Definition: Saying something is better or correct just because it has always been done that way.

Example: “We should keep using paper worksheets because we’ve always used them.”

10. Ad Hominem

Definition: Attacking the person instead of their argument.

Example: “You’re wrong about recycling because you’re too young.”

11. Tu Quoque (“You too”)

Definition: Avoiding the argument by pointing out that the other person did the same thing.

Example: “You can’t tell me to clean my room because your room is messy too!”

12. Red Herring

Definition: Changing the subject to distract from the real issue.

Example: “We shouldn’t worry about pollution because so many people litter anyway.”
